Human Factors in Design Project

Part 1: Evaluating In-Game Application Usability Using UTSE

Problem Definition

One of the Core Key Issues that we that we expected to face during the development of our application, was could this application be used mid game by the background staff to inform each other of a players potential injury that suffered.

When carrying out our UTSE we had three potential users that could be using the application mid game:

  • The Manager
  • The Coach(es)
  • The Physio(s)

During the development of this application, there were many potential human factors issues that we were wary about such as:

  • Location where is the application being used:
    • Are they at home by the fireside
    • Are they in their office
    • Is it being used in training
    • Is it being used mid game (if its being used mid game what is the atmosphere like, what are the weather conditions like)
  • The User - and issues they may have using the application:
    • Will their age cause an issue
    • Are they tech savvy
    • Have they any visual impairments
    • Have they any Physical impairments
UI Redesign

A recurring theme among the challenges faced, including cognitive load, ergonomic considerations, and human error/performance, centres around the responsibilities of the manager/physio during a match. This underscores the necessity of adapting the application's interface to better accommodate these demands.

Solution: Implementing Match Mode for the app to use during matches

The Match Mode will feature a simplified UI mode which will only feature specific functionalities that the manager/physio/coach will use during a match. A button/switch will be implemented in the UI so that the manager/physio can click/toggle on it to switch to this mode when a match is starting.

Activating Match Mode

There would be several ways to activate this mode:

  1. Activate it manually from the settings screen
  2. Based on the time and location of the match set on the schedule. If the app detects that the manager is in the location of the match and the match is almost starting,
    • A pop-up (if user is in-app) or a notification (if user is not in-app) prompting if the user wants to turn on match mode will show up.
    • A floating button will also show up for the manager to toggle between modes

Having multiple ways for the user to activate the Match Mode in the app will give them more flexibility to seamlessly integrate Match Mode into their workflow, ensuring they can access essential functionalities with ease and efficiency.

Match Mode UI Overview

Features

The features that will be included in this Match Mode will differ based on the role.

The functionalities for Manager will include:

  • Recording the total duration played for each player during the match
  • Choosing players for substitution

The functionalities for Physio will include:

  • Flagging an injured player for further check up

Look and Feel

  • In crowded stadiums with adverse weather conditions, the UI should have high-contrast colours and large, easy-to-read text to ensure visibility even in poor lighting.
    • This would also help if the user using the app has accessibility issues such as visually impaired, colour blindness, and visual perception.
  • Dark mode feature can also be implemented to adjust the colour scheme for better readability in low-light environments for night matches.

Gestures

  • To enhance usability during match conditions, it's crucial to consider the gestures required for app interaction. This entails thoughtful considerations, such as:
    • Strategically positioning buttons to align with natural hand movements
    • Incorporating intuitive gestures that facilitate seamless navigation and interaction with the app

Manager View

  • To simplify the manager's workflow when recording player's duration, automatic calculation of it can be implemented in the app.
  • Main view is presented based on the formation of the players on the pitch. As the manager would be familiar with this, it would make it easier for them to find the player they would want to select.
  • Manager can choose the player they would want to substitute then select the substitute player they want to bring in from the list.
    • The list of substitutes will be sorted based on their position. The players with the same position with the player being replaced will appear on top as they will most likely be the one being chosen,
  • After choosing, the confirmation page will show up and the manager can click confirm when the substitution happens, this will then record the time the player went off the pitch and also record the time the substitute went in the pitch.
    • Calculation can be done in the system using data from the other recorded time related to the match (match start time, match end time, half time).

Physio View

  • During a match, when a player is injured, the physio will need to change the player's status to unavailable
  • Physio will select the injured player then will be asked to confirm if this is the player that is injured to minimise human error.
  • This will then notify the manager and coach that the player is unavailable.
  • It will also notify the physio to remind themselves to schedule further check up with the player to assess the injury.

Design and Interaction

  • Contrasting colours are vital for this app especially during Match Mode as the conditions can make it hard for the user to see, and it is also vital for users with visual impairments.
    • Contrast checking the colours before using them is essential to do and can be done here.

    • * Note: The colour green and blue failed one of the normal text tests but it does not matter in this context as it is only going to be used for large texts and UI components

  • In addition to contrasting colours, we will also include icons and text descriptions for the traffic light system to accommodate for colour blindness

  • The decision to position two buttons, one on the far left and the other on the far right at the bottom of the page for confirm and cancel functions, is based on mimicking natural hand movements. Typically, users hold their phones with one hand and use their thumb for navigation. Placing the buttons in these locations facilitates easier access for the user. Moreover, the placement of the confirm button on the right side highlights on the fact that most people are right-handed, making it more reachable and aligning with its frequent usage.

  • The app also incorporates intuitive back gestures for seamless navigation. Users have the option to utilise either the traditional back button located in the top left corner or simply swipe right to go back. This dual approach caters to diverse user preferences, providing flexibility and ease of navigation within the app interface.

Part 2: User-Focused Analysis of the Manager Role

Problem Definition

The original problem we had was seeing how well our application could be used during matchday conditions to perform certain functions such as calculating a player's match duration and updating player's injury history.

For this stage of the project we will be taking the improvements made by our design specialist which was to have a match day screen mode that would limit the amount of steps needed to perform these aforementioned functions.

Our goals with this test is to provide a report that answers the simple question can our application be used in matchday (or training) environment.

UTSE Analysis

  1. User

    • Name: John O'Brien
    • Age: 59
    • Position: Manager of Clann Eireann GAA Team

    At 59 years old, John O'Brien has an imposing demeanour that reflects his extensive expertise in the Gaelic Athletic Association (GAA). Standing 6 feet tall, he exudes authority and confidence, which naturally attracts attention. Despite his age, John maintains a strong and athletic physique, demonstrating his commitment to health and the activity he enjoys. Wants to be able to track his players easily without it being overwhelmed.

  2. System

    In ordinary circumstances John would typically be using a clipboard pen and paper, however as this is the championship final John will using a iPad Pro 6th Generation that was given to him by the club

  3. Task

    John's task is simple, he wants to be able to use the app to keep track of players that he has substituted off either because of an injury or he due to player fatigue (which could potentially lead to injuries)

  4. Environment

    It's the 2nd half of the championship final which is being held in the Athletics Grounds in Armagh, Its a cold overcast and wet night in a stadium filled with 18,000 people and the floodlights shining down on pitch. One of John's players has gone down and holding their head after a collision with an opposing teams player. John is sitting in the dugout and due to the heavy fog and rain is unable to recognise which one of his players it is that is injured, however as he starts to use the app he begins walking out to the technical with in order to get a better view of which one of his players is done while the raucous crowd jeering and booing behind him.

Nielsen and Molich’s User Interface Guidelines

In order to further improve the usability, utility, and desirability of my design, I decided to look into Nielsen and Molich’s ten user interface guidelines [1] and do an analysis on how my design adheres to some of the guidelines, and make appropriate changes or recommendations to make it adhere to the corresponding guideline.

Visibility of System Status

Whenever users interact with a system, they need to know whether the interaction was successful [2]. Confirmation of user action is a part of having a visibility of system status. A way this could be implemented in this design is to provide a confirmation after the manager has successfully made a substitution. On mobile applications, this is commonly done using Snackbars. The Snackbar can show text such as: "Substitution successful!".

System Match to the Real World

Users expect that UI elements which reflect material objects be similar to those objects from the real world [3]. While designing the wireframe, my focus was on how the user interface should match real life situations during a match, which can also be seen reflected in the prototype, especially the main screen. The main screen of "Match Mode" shows each player on the pitch in their respective positions on the pitch, which would be something that the manager would be really familiar with. This will help them in choosing the player they want to take off as it will make them easier to find the player they have in mind. Having other visual cues like pictures, number, and last name clearly shown will also give more context to the player in that position.

User Control and Freedom

Part of a great user experience is nurturing users’ feeling of control over the user interface (UI) they happen to be using. Users should be able to quickly correct mistakes or backtrack on choices made. [4] Currently, the design has implemented back buttons and a slide right gesture to allow the user to go back to the previous page if for example they selected the wrong player. In the confirmation page the manager also have the option to cancel this step if they made a mistake in choosing a player they are going to put in. Other than this automatic mode, it would also be beneficial to have the manager revise the time manually during normal mode as they might make mistakes and was not on time when they do this action.

Error Prevention

Nielsen mentioned that a way to prevent error is to check for them and present users with a confirmation option before they commit to the action [5]. This is implemented in my prototype, specifically the confirmation page where the manager needs to either confirm or cancel the substitution being made. Another potential error that the manager might make is an error when choosing the player they want to take off from the main screen. A way to prevent this is to only allow player selection after a long tap/long press. It adds a layer of confirmation to the action, minimising potential errors. The trade off is that it might cause a delay as this is a process that needs to be timely. A way to figure out going forward is by conducting user testing on this feature.

Recognition rather than Recall

In my prototype design, I implemented this by giving the manager clear visuals on the main screen using the players image, number, position, and also their surnames. I also made sure that the information shown (image, number, surname) is consistent throughout the other pages where the players are shown to help them recognise the players and would not have to depend on their memory to recall which player they want to select as they have clear visuals on the players' details.

Aesthetic and Minimalist Design

Interfaces should not contain information that is irrelevant or rarely needed [5]. In the design of my prototype, I implemented this principle to only have needed information to keep the UI clean and easy to look at. In the main match page, I only included the players on the pitch and a button to exit the mode, keeping it focused on the pitch itself. On the second page, the substitute screen, I included only relevant information such as the player selected and the players available for substitution. Same thing with the confirmation page, I provided the user with only the essential information, which are a visualisation of who is being taken off and who is being sent in and buttons to cancel or confirm.

Help and Documentation

Currently, there are no tutorials or documentation helping the manager on how to navigate around the "Match Mode" UI, but we do have some of this implemented in our "normal" mode of the app, providing a guidebook and a set of getting started screens. Implementing this for the new mode will definitely be beneficial for the manager to streamline their learning process of the new mode, as it is quite different from the "normal" mode they are used to. Providing guided step by step tutorial showcasing specific features of the app in-app during the first time the manager opens the mode would also be something that would be nice to have as it shows a more detailed step-by-step overview of the new features in this mode.

One Thumb and One Eyeball

In a distracted environment, such as what PlayMetrix is going to be used for in this context, which is a pitch during a high intensity match, the best form of smartphone interaction is a high-speed, easy-to-use one [6]. A test such as the one thumb and one eyeball test, or in other words "Can users perform a certain number of tasks with just one hand in under 60 seconds?" would be a suitable method to test the app for this situation.

© Interaction Design Foundation, CC BY-SA 4.0

In designing the UI, it is important that the essential buttons, or the buttons that the user will use the most would be in the user's "easy" comfort zone to allow the user to reach it easily and therefore making the task easier to complete. I initially assumed in the wireframe that it would make more sense for the "Confirm" button should be on the right side to accommodate the majority right-handed people who will hold their phone using their right hand, but after seeing the Hand-reach Comfort Zone by the Interaction Design Foundation, I was proven wrong as most of the hand-reach comfort zone for right hand users would actually be on the left side of the screen.

To analyse how the confirmation screen adheres to this, I overlayed the hand-reach comfort zone to the screen and it can be seen that both buttons are within the "easy" zone, which is a good thing. But it can be seen that the "Confirm" button is nearer to the border of the "Ok" zone, while the "Cancel" button is in a more central position. This would make the "Cancel" button easier to reach than the "Confirm" button, which is not what we want as the manager would more likely to "Confirm" than to "Cancel".

After knowing this information, I think it would be appropriate to make some minor changes on the prototype by switching the position of the "Cancel" and "Confirm" button in the confirmation page, resulting in a final prototype as seen below:

Overall Findings

Problem Addressed

Initial

Initially, our problem was how PlayMetrix would be used during a match, this includes two key user roles, the manager and the physio. The manager would use the app to record the duration played by players and the physio would use the app to record injuries/edit player availability mid-game.

Interim

We refined the problem scope to only focus on the manager recording the duration during a live match and how the manager can use PlayMetrix to do this task.

Final

Going from and the interim problem definition, we wanted to focus on how the wireframe developed from CA1 can be used for these match day conditions for a manager to record duration played by a player and analyse it from a human error and performance, cognitive, ergonomics, accessibility, and interaction perspective.

Key Human Factors Issue

Human Errors & Performance

  • Live Match Performance - If the manager substitutes the wrong player on or off, what can be done to remedy this.
  • Data loss - The effects of data loss for the manager.

Cognitive

  • Reduced short-term and long-term memory load.
  • Increased efficiency and accuracy.
  • Improved user experience for managers.
  • Better data recording for informed decision-making.
  • Reduces the amount of cognitive load/error on John.
  • I recommend for a manager to use this "match mode" on match day

Ergonomics

  • Location - Where the user is i.e stadium or pitch, where they are in that stadium are they in the dugout, the technical area or banished to the stands, are they standing or sitting
  • Weather - Clear, Dry, Overcast, Rainy, Thunder, Snow, different conditions may prove to have different outcomes
  • Device - If the user has a small screen can they see all the functions or have they to scroll/navigate to them. If the devices screen is wet will that cause misinputs, if the sun is hitting the screen will the user be able to see the screen, if the battery is dying then have they to be conservative in how they use the app
  • The User - How are they holding the device, are the looking at it in a way that can cause neck strain, according to Colorado State gripping the tablet with a key pinch (where the finger and thumb awkwardly grip the device can cause injury if done for extended periods of time

Accessibility

  • Red and green color similarity for Protanopia - The similarities between red and green for Protanopia make both colours almost impossible to distinguish between. This can pose a challenge for a manager who may suffer from Protanopia colour blindness which may affect their ability to use the app when making a substitution.
  • Impact of distorted grey shades - The colour grey across all types of colour blindness that was analysed showed that the colour grey was perceived has different variations and shades of grey depending on the type of colour blindness. The distortion of grey may hinder the manager's ability to identify the player being taken off the field, this added complexity can hinder a manager's efficiency if they suffer from colour blindness.

Interaction

  • Lack of help/guidance - The match mode UI does not have any resources or tutorials the manager can refer to when they are first getting started.
  • Placement of buttons - In the confirmation screen, the button for the "confirm" is placed on a more unsuitable position on the screen based on the hand-reach comfort zone even though it is the "default" button the manager wants.
  • No confirmation shown after successful action - This might make the users unsure whether their task was done successfully or not.

Re-Design Recommendations

Human Errors & Performance

  • Provide clear and immediate feedback to managers for every action they perform, especially in critical operations like substitution.
  • Use visual cues, such as colour-coded alerts or notifications, to indicate errors, warnings, or successful actions.
  • Provide an undo functionality to undo substitutions.
  • Default to MatchMode during a live match

Cognitive

  • Allow audio input to take players off during e.g. "Take Michael off"
  • Offer pre populated "subs" section so the manager doesn't have to think of who can play instead.
  • Post match review - visual graph of analytics so the manager knows how the team performed

Ergonomics

  • Recommended Sub Features would prevent the user scrolling for a player to find
  • A case that has something on the back like a pop socket for the user to grip on instead of awkwardly holding the tablet which could cause injury
  • Screencast mode to project to on a larger screen so the user isn't straining their neck by looking down
  • Noise Cancelling Headphones that blocks out the physical distractions such as crowds and opposition

Accessibility

  • Having a customisation colour blindness profile filter in the settings of the app allows the manager to be able to change how the colours are perceived based on the type of colour blindness they may suffer from. This setting when applied should be applied across all screens for the app for the manager.

Interaction

  • Provide useful tutorials and documentations - This can be in the form of an online guidebook and/or in-app interactive tutorials.
  • Switch the placement of buttons - The "confirm" and "cancel" button positions should be switched to make it more natural for the manager to click the "confirm" instead of "cancel".
  • Show confirmation after successful action - This would highlight the system status and give user reassurance that they successfully did the task.

Benefits/Improvements

Human Errors & Performance

  • Match Mode Undo can reduce Managers' panic if they substitute incorrectly or change their minds.
  • Recovery of data will reduce stress on Managers.
  • Allows Managers' to focus on the game by reducing steps to activate Match Mode.

Cognitive

  • Match mode significantly reduces memory load by taking away the need for calculations
  • 3 less steps to carry out which reduces the load on the manager.
  • Visuals help with the memory with having profile pictures in the match line up
  • Helps the manager with the cognitive load we can control

Ergonomics

  • Matchday Mode helps making tasks for the manager simpler
  • The larger device make it easier to read the screen better
  • The Cover on the screen helps prevent it from getting wet and prevents misinputs
  • A UV Screen Protector prevents Bright Lights from making the screen eligible
  • Having a 100% charged device means there is no fear it having to use it conservatively

Accessibility

  • The use of iconography like the 'tick' on the border of the substitute players identifies that a player is available. This improvement makes it easier for the manager to understand that a player is available by the visual representation of the 'tick' regardless of colour.
  • The use of text labels like 'OUT' and 'IN' is great to indicate what player is being substituted off and what player is being substituted on regardless of colour.

Interaction

  • A big improvement in the interaction aspect is that the whole "Match Mode" really simplifies the interactions and steps needed for the manager to record the duration as they would not need to input anything, they just need to click on buttons and it only takes 3 taps.

Limitations

Human Errors & Performance

  • Managers' may change their mind about substitutions during the match.
  • Managers' may not want Match Mode by default during Live Matches.

Cognitive

  • Subbing people can be confusing as the manager has to think on the spot - whereas if the manager can prepare the list in order he wants and it automatically subs that person on it would reduce the load massively.

Ergonomics

  • Can get easily overwhelmed by physical distractions such as the crowd or bright lights
  • Poor Weather Conditions could cause misinputs

Accessibility

  • If a manager with colour blindness relies strongly on visuals it may cause some confusion when using the app as the manager will perceive colours differently
  • The grey colour distortions depending on the type of colour blindness may cause challenges for the manager identifying players in the team lineout screen

Interaction

  • A limitation in the current implementation of the match mode is that the manager will need to be accurate time-wise when performing the action. It might be hard to ensure perfect accuracy in the timing of the substitution due to the other factors happening and it might be a busy situation on the sideline when a substitution is made. A recommendation to minimise this is by also having a manual duration input in the normal mode so the manager can fix the duration later on if any inaccuracies are made.

What We Learned

Human Errors & Performance

  • Manager error is inevitable so allowing them to rectify this is crucial.
  • Managers' may choose to sub on limited/unavailable players.
  • Managers' may accidentally exit match mode with easy slip button on the bottom.

Cognitive

  • The needs for the manager to calculate their own time while our application can perform this for them is a massive advantage.
  • Reducing tasks for the manager to carry out also helps massively with cognitive overload.
  • Visuals and pictures can help with reducing cognitive load
  • Applying guidelines to design is crucial for ease of user engagement.

Ergonomics

  • The location of the user is crucial to how successfully they could use the app, in the comfort of their dugout they could perform the task quicker and easier then when they were stood out in the technical area
  • The Type of the device and how you're holding it also can also effect performance,
  • Weather Conditions will effect how successful the user is in completing tasks on the application

Accessibility

  • How different colours are perceived across all types of colour blindness can be significantly different depending on the type of colour blindness.
  • However, with significant visual changes perceived by someone with colour blindness, some colours can be perceived as very similar to each other Examples of this are red and green for someone who may suffer from Protanopia.

Interaction

  • I really had an interesting deep probe on Nielsen and Molich’s ten user interface guidelines and I feel like these guidelines really provide clear and intuitive guidelines on how to design websites with better usability.
  • I also did some research on how people mostly interact with their phone, for example from the way they hold it, and it is important to adjust your design based on this.

How Human Factors Impacted the Design

After doing a deep probe on a specific interaction (manager recording duration) and considering the human factors related to that, we really had a lot of insights on how to design applications that are made for humans and consider aspects such as ergonomics, cognitive, human error and performance, as well as designing for accessibility. This led us into realising that there are a lot of things that we need to consider when designing an application's interface as human are complex creatures and each and every one of us perceive things differently.

What makes sense to one person might not make sense to others, so following standards and guidelines while designing an interface is important to ensure that our app's design is usable for other people and not causing frustration. User testing is also an important aspect in this as we want to have feedback from real humans and understand how they perceive our application from their point of view. Overall, by incorporating user feedback and considering human factors throughout the design process, we can create interfaces that are intuitive, efficient, and enjoyable for a diverse range of users.
